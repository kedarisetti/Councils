# Remarkability — Reference Concepts

Primary source: Seth Godin, *Purple Cow: Transform Your Business by Being Remarkable* (2003)
Secondary: Derek Sivers on "hell yeah or no"; Everett Rogers, *Diffusion of Innovations*

---

## The Core Test

**"Will people who encounter this remark on it to someone who wasn't there?"**

This is behavioral, not attitudinal. Not "will they like it?" Not "will they use it?"
— will they *tell someone else about it*? The test is behavioral because propagation
is behavioral. An idea that generates positive internal assessment but no external
communication is, for practical purposes, invisible.

---

## Purple Cow Framework (Godin)

**The metaphor:** Driving through the countryside, you see cows. Hundreds of cows.
At first they're interesting. Then they're not. But a *purple cow* — you'd stop the
car. You'd photograph it. You'd tell people about it that evening.

A purple cow is not just good. It is *remarkable* — worth making a remark about.

**What makes something remarkable vs. merely good:**

Remarkable is a function of contrast against category norms, not absolute quality.
A remarkable thing is remarkable *relative to what people expect to see in this category.*

This has a counterintuitive implication: being very good in a category full of very
good things is not remarkable. You need to be different in a way that registers.

**The smoothing problem:** Godin's observation is that most organizations optimize for
"no one will complain" — and produce things that no one will discuss. The committee
process, the stakeholder review, the effort to appeal to the broadest possible audience
— all of these are smoothing mechanisms. They eliminate the edge cases, the weird
configurations, the choices that could alienate someone. Those choices are often exactly
where the propagation energy lives.

**Safe is risky.** The most dangerous position for an idea is the "safe middle" —
good enough to not fail visibly, but not distinctive enough to spread. These ideas die
slowly and invisibly. The apparently risky position (a distinctive idea that some people
will dislike) often spreads to exactly the people for whom it is designed.

## Domain Translation of Remarkability

The underlying test ("will this propagate?") holds across all domains. The mechanism
of propagation differs by domain.

### Product / Business
- Propagation mechanism: word-of-mouth, social sharing, unprompted recommendation
- Test: "Would you tell a friend about this?" (without being asked or incentivized)
- Purple cow signals: surprising feature, unexpectedly delightful experience, does
  something competitors explicitly decided not to do

### Research / Academic
- Propagation mechanism: citations, conference presentations, "I read this paper and..."
- Test: Does this force other researchers to update their priors? Would it make a
  good conference talk title?
- Purple cow signals: overturns an assumption the field holds, opens a new research
  direction, produces a result that experts find surprising
- Note: "Surprising but holds up" is the research purple cow. "Surprising but wrong"
  propagates but damages.

### Mathematics
- Propagation mechanism: citations, textbook inclusion, named theorems/conjectures
- Test: Does this produce a new distinction or technique that other mathematicians
  will want to use? Is it counterintuitive in a way that holds under scrutiny?
- Purple cow signals: connects two areas previously thought separate, provides an
  unexpectedly simple proof of a hard result, reveals structure nobody knew was there

### Policy / Social Ideas
- Propagation mechanism: adoption by practitioners, policy papers, media coverage,
  changed discourse
- Test: Does this reframe how people talk about the problem? Does the old framing
  feel inadequate after hearing this one?
- Purple cow signals: produces a memorable concept or metaphor, explains why previous
  solutions failed, makes a counterintuitive but defensible claim about the problem

### Foundational / Philosophical
- Propagation mechanism: referenced in other works, produces new questions and research
- Test: Does it produce a new distinction that didn't exist before? Does it change
  what questions people ask?
- Purple cow signals: counterintuitive but coherent, opens territory that was previously
  inaccessible, names something that was previously unnamed

## The "Brown Cow" Version

For any idea, there is a brown cow version: safe, good, unremarkable, forgettable.
It reaches the same destination but generates no conversation.

Examples:
- Brown cow: "A new project management tool with better UI" → Purple cow: "A project
  management tool that deletes your tasks if you don't act on them within 24 hours"
- Brown cow: "A proof that this algorithm runs in O(n log n) time" → Purple cow: "A proof
  that no comparison sort can do better than O(n log n), which means the algorithm is
  already optimal — there's nothing left to find"
- Brown cow: "A policy proposal to improve education outcomes" → Purple cow: "A proposal
  that giving students veto power over curriculum produces better outcomes than any
  tested curriculum redesign"

The exercise of constructing the brown cow version is diagnostic: if the current idea
and its brown cow version are hard to distinguish, the smoothing problem is real.

## Derek Sivers — "Hell Yeah or No"

Sivers' contribution: the remarkability test applied from the *creator's* perspective,
not the audience's.

**The test:** If something isn't a "hell yeah!" from you — if you're not excited enough
to tell people about it yourself — say no.

**Why this matters for ideas:** If the person developing the idea cannot articulate
what is remarkable about it in a way that excites them, there are two possibilities:
1. The idea has been smoothed and the person no longer sees what was distinctive about it
2. The idea was never remarkable from the inside, which predicts it won't be remarkable
   from the outside

**Applied in evaluation:** Ask the idea's proponent to complete the sentence: "What
makes this remarkable is..." If they struggle, or produce a generic answer, that is a
signal.

## Rogers — Diffusion of Innovations (Propagation Mechanics)

Everett Rogers' framework for why some innovations spread and others don't. Five
attributes that predict propagation:

1. **Relative advantage**: Is it better than the alternative? (Necessary but not sufficient)
2. **Compatibility**: Does it fit existing values and practices? (Higher = faster diffusion)
3. **Complexity**: How hard is it to understand and use? (Lower = faster)
4. **Trialability**: Can people try it without full commitment? (Higher = faster)
5. **Observability**: Can people see others using it? (Higher = faster)

**The remarkability connection:** Godin's purple cow addresses mainly relative advantage
and observability. An idea can be high on relative advantage but low on observability —
it works better but people can't tell that it does. The remarkability analysis should
account for all five: even a remarkable idea fails to propagate if it is incompatible
with existing practices or impossible to try.

## Smoothness Failure Modes Checklist

| Mechanism | Description | Signal |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Broadening | Expanded appeal until no one is excited | Generic target audience description |
| Edge removal | Distinctive features removed to reduce friction | "We softened X because some users might not like it" |
| Burial | The most interesting part described as secondary | Key insight in footnote or appendix |
| Language homogenization | Distinctive vocabulary replaced with category-default terms | Could describe any 5 competitors |
| Safety optimization | Every choice that "could go wrong" was avoided | No decision with visible downside risk |
| Consensus | Review process averaged out all strong opinions | No one owns the most distinctive element |
