# Steven Johnson — *Where Good Ideas Come From* — Reference

Source: Steven Johnson, *Where Good Ideas Come From: The Natural History of Innovation* (2010)

---

## The Adjacent Possible

**Definition:** At any moment, there is a bounded set of next moves available to a
system — biological, cultural, or technological. This is the adjacent possible: things
that *could* exist given what currently exists, but don't yet. It is exactly one step
away from the actual.

**The "expanding house" metaphor:** The adjacent possible is like a house that keeps
expanding as you open doors. Each room you enter reveals new rooms that were inaccessible
from your starting position. Crucially, you cannot skip rooms — you must pass through
each one in sequence. The metaphor is not about infinite possibility; it is about
*constrained* possibility that expands as you explore it.

**Why timing matters:** An idea outside the adjacent possible is not wrong — it is
premature. Babbage's Analytical Engine was conceptually sound in 1837 but outside the
adjacent possible because precision machining, electrical components, and manufacturing
scale hadn't been developed. The idea required rooms that didn't exist yet.

**The history of innovation reframed:** Innovation is not primarily the history of genius.
It is the history of the adjacent possible expanding. Most "breakthroughs" were
simultaneous inventions — multiple people reached the same idea at the same time because
the adjacent possible had just opened that room for everyone.

**Precondition types:**
- Technological: components, materials, hardware, software
- Behavioral: critical mass of adoption, learned behaviors, cultural shifts
- Economic: cost curves falling to viability thresholds
- Regulatory: legal frameworks, property rights, liability structures
- Infrastructure: platforms, networks, ecosystems that didn't exist before

## The Slow Hunch

**Definition:** Most breakthrough ideas do not arrive as sudden flashes. They develop
over months or years as a vague sense that something interesting is here, without a clear
shape yet. Darwin circled natural selection for years before articulating it fully.

**Why incubation is necessary:** A slow hunch is incomplete. It's missing a component
or a frame. The completion often happens through an unexpected collision — with another
person's idea, a piece of reading, a failed experiment, or an unrelated observation.
The hunch needs time and exposure to find what it's missing.

**Slow hunch vs. weak idea — diagnostic markers:**
- A slow hunch keeps *returning* across different, disconnected contexts. You think
  you've moved on, but something unrelated triggers it again.
- A slow hunch generates productive confusion — real questions, not dead ends.
- A slow hunch has a "not yet" quality, not a "this doesn't work" quality. Under
  examination it survives but can't yet fully articulate itself.
- A slow hunch attaches to other ideas — you mentally connect it to things in different
  domains unprompted.
- A weak idea collapses under the first serious examination.

**Practical implication:** Don't rush a slow hunch into implementation before it's
complete. The missing piece will arrive — the question is whether you've created the
conditions for it to arrive. Keeping a commonplace book (second brain, knowledge garden)
is the classic mechanism: write hunches down, revisit them, allow collisions.

## Exaptation

**Definition:** A feature or mechanism that evolved (or was designed) for one purpose
gets repurposed for a completely different one. From evolutionary biology (Gould and Vrba,
1982): structures originally shaped by natural selection for a function are "exapted" —
co-opted for a different function.

**The Gutenberg example:** Gutenberg did not invent most of what made the printing press.
He combined three existing mechanisms from entirely different domains:
- The screw press (from wine/olive pressing — agriculture)
- Oil-based ink (from painting)
- Movable type (from coin-stamping and seals)

The analytical move Gutenberg made was not invention but *transfer of mechanism across
domains*. The screw press was exapted from agriculture.

**Other examples:**
- Internet packet-switching: designed for military fault-tolerance (ARPA), exapted for
  commercial networking
- GPS timing signals: designed for military navigation, exapted for financial transaction
  timestamping, then for mobile location services
- Bird feathers: evolved for temperature regulation, exapted for flight

**Why exaptation beats invention:** Starting from scratch requires developing every
component. Exaptation leverages mechanisms that have already been proven in another
context. The R&D cost is zero — the mechanism exists; you're paying only for the transfer.

**The analytical move:** For any new idea, ask: what is the *function* this idea needs
to perform? Then search for existing mechanisms that perform that function in another
domain. The less obvious the domain, the more valuable the exaptation (because competitors
are less likely to have found it).

## Liquid Networks

**Definition:** Idea generation is an environmental condition, not a solo cognitive
event. A "liquid" network is one that is dense enough for ideas to collide and recombine,
but not so rigid (solid) that ideas are locked in place, and not so chaotic (gas) that
ideas can't connect at all.

**The three states:**
- **Solid network:** Ideas are locked in place. Rigid hierarchies, disciplinary silos,
  isolated teams. Nothing new forms because there are no collisions.
- **Gas network:** Too much randomness. Ideas bounce around without connecting. Chaos
  without structure.
- **Liquid network:** Dense enough for collisions, structured enough for connections to
  persist. This is where innovation happens.

**Conditions that enable liquid networks:**
- **Weak ties matter more than strong ties.** Strong ties (close colleagues, tight teams)
  share the same information. Weak ties (acquaintances, adjacent disciplines) carry
  genuinely novel signals.
- **Cross-domain exposure.** Ideas from biology collide with ideas from economics →
  evolutionary game theory. Confinement within a single discipline is a liquid-network
  failure.
- **Unstructured collision time.** Deliberate seminars and working groups are less
  productive for innovation than ambient collision spaces — the 18th-century coffeehouse,
  the Bell Labs cafeteria. The structure forces topics; the ambient space allows accidents.

**Why this matters for evaluating ideas:** An idea stuck in a solid network (single team,
single domain, single discipline) will not develop the connections it needs. Part of the
evaluation of any idea is: what is the coffeehouse analog — what environment would allow
this idea to collide with the other ideas it needs to become complete?

## Error as Innovation Driver

**Johnson's claim:** Noise, errors, and accidental results are disproportionately
responsible for breakthroughs. The ability to recognize a *productive error* — to see
an unexpected result as a signal rather than a failure — is itself an innovation skill.

**Examples:**
- **Penicillin** (Fleming): contaminated petri dish (bacterial culture killed by mold).
  The error was noticed rather than discarded.
- **Vulcanized rubber** (Goodyear): accidentally dropped rubber-sulfur mixture on a
  hot stove. Recognized the result as useful.
- **X-rays** (Röntgen): stray cathode rays unexpectedly exposing photographic plates.
  Investigated rather than discarded.

**The asymmetry:** Most processes optimize to minimize and eliminate error. Innovation
requires a second channel: a practice of asking "is this error *interesting*?" before
discarding it. This is not tolerating sloppiness — it is curating anomalies.

**Applied to idea evaluation:** Before designing this idea from scratch, ask: what
unexpected results have adjacent attempts produced? What have people tried that "failed"
but produced interesting anomalies? The anomalies in prior failures often contain the
missing component for the current idea.

## The Commonplace Book (Knowledge Collider)

Johnson's recommendation for individuals: maintain a "commonplace book" — a running
record of ideas, hunches, quotations, observations, and connections across domains. Not
organized by topic but by time of encounter, so that ideas from different periods and
domains collide on the same page.

The modern equivalent: a second brain / knowledge garden (Roam, Obsidian, Notion). The
mechanism matters less than the practice: capture hunches before they fade, revisit them
regularly, and create conditions for unexpected collisions between ideas you captured at
different times.

**Why this matters for slow hunches:** The slow hunch that never gets written down is
the one that gets forgotten between recurrences. Writing it down keeps it alive through
the incubation period.
